Closing iron is a skill.

Crush grip is more nervous system than muscle. Closing a heavier gripper is about teaching your hand to recruit harder, faster, and from a more disadvantageous start position — not just growing forearms.

"You don't get stronger by closing grippers. You close grippers because you got stronger." — train the system, test with the gripper.
The Five Pillars
What this masterclass is built on
▶
1 · Set position is everything
A wider start position = a harder rep. Most failed closes happen because the gripper isn't sunk deep into the palm before the squeeze starts. Set technique often adds a full CoC level for free.
2 · Strength is specific to ROM
Closing the last quarter inch is a different skill than starting the squeeze. You must train both — but the close is where almost everyone fails. Negatives + overcrush work end-range.
3 · Wave the intensity
Crush is highly neural. You can't grind it daily without frying the hands. Heavy days, light days, deload weeks. Wave it like a deadlift program, not like curls.
4 · Train the supporting cast
Pinch grip, thick-bar work, finger extensors, and wrists are not optional. They prevent injury and unlock the crush. Skip them and you'll plateau by Trainer or No. 1.
5 · Test rarely. Train often.
A true MMS (Mean Metallic Set) attempt is a 1RM. Don't test every session. Test every 4–6 weeks. Between tests, train at 70–90% of your gripper.
What "closing" actually means
The rules
▶
The Standard
  • Handles touch — no daylight between them.
  • From a parallel set — handles parallel to your forearm before you squeeze. No "credit card" assists, no table presses, no two-hand sets.
  • Held for 1 second at minimum for a clean close.
Common "Cheats"
  • Credit card set: Use a card to push the handle in deeper before gripping. Builds confidence and end-range strength but isn't a certified close.
  • Deep set: Use your off-hand to crush the handles closer before transferring. Same — training tool, not a real close.
  • MMS: Mean Metallic Set — handles set parallel without any assist. This is the certifiable close.
Train with credit-card and deep sets to push end-range strength. Test with MMS.

Buying strategy
Which to own
▶
If you're starting fresh
Buy Trainer, No. 1, and No. 2. These three cover years of progress. Add No. 0.5 if you find the Trainer-to-No.1 gap brutal (it usually is).
If you're past No. 2
Buy No. 1.5, No. 2, and No. 2.5. The 1.5 becomes your high-rep/volume tool, the 2 your "working" gripper, and 2.5 your stretch goal. Skipping a level wider is the #1 mistake here.
Don't buy the No. 3 yet
Until you can do 5+ clean MMS reps with the No. 2 and credit-card a No. 2.5, the No. 3 is just gym décor. Save the money for a hand expander and pinch blocks.

The Set

Your set position determines how strong you are. Every gripper closer has a set, and they've drilled it like a deadlift setup.

The MMS Set, step by step
The certifiable parallel set
▶
1
Plant the bottom handle in the palm
Bottom handle sits deep across the base of your thumb — the soft fleshy heel of the palm — angled diagonally. Not across the calluses at the base of your fingers. Wrong placement loses you a full level of strength.
2
Wrap the pinky low
Pinky finger wraps as close to the bottom of the top handle as possible. Pinky leverage matters more than index leverage on hard grippers.
3
Stagger the fingers
Index slightly higher, then middle, ring, pinky in a slight diagonal — naturally formed by the angle of your palm. Fingers should not be parallel to each other across the handle.
4
Lock the wrist neutral
Wrist straight or very slightly extended. A flexed wrist (curled toward you) cuts your grip strength dramatically. Think "long, straight forearm."
5
Set to parallel
Before the real squeeze starts, the handles should be parallel to your forearm. You can use your off hand to position — but it can't help close. Once set, off-hand drops.
6
Squeeze — fingers first, then thumb
Drive the four fingers into the palm. The thumb adds force at the end. Don't lead with the thumb — it's the weakest link and biases the wrist.
7
Crush, don't twitch
A full, accelerating crush from start to close. If you stop at the sticking point, you lose. Mental cue: "drive it through the close, hold the touch."
Heel of palm → pinky low → wrist neutral → set parallel → fingers first → accelerate through.
Hand position cheats (training only)
For overload work
▶
Credit-card set
Insert a credit card between handles. Close partially with the card in place. Remove and finish. Excellent for end-range overload — you handle a gripper one level above MMS-ready.
Deep set (table-assisted)
Use the off hand or a table edge to push handles closer before squeezing. Lets you train the final close on a much heavier gripper than you can MMS.
Negatives (eccentrics)
Close with two hands or external help, then slowly resist as it opens — 5–10 seconds. The single best tool for breaking through to the next gripper.
Overcrushes
Close a gripper, then keep squeezing for 3–10 seconds. Trains end-range tension and confidence with the closed position.
Rule: Cheat sets train. MMS sets test. Don't confuse them. Logging a credit-card close as a "close" is how you stay stuck.
Common technique faults
Diagnose & fix
▶
Bottom handle too high in the palm
Handle sits across calluses → tiny lever arm, hand opens at the bottom. Push it deeper into the meaty heel.
Wrist flexion (curling toward forearm)
As fatigue hits, the wrist breaks. Grip drops 20%+ instantly. Cue: "long forearm, straight wrist." Train wrist extension/curl as accessory.
Thumb-leading squeeze
The thumb is the weakest digit. If it fires first, the fingers wait. Fingers drive the crush; thumb anchors at the end.
Hesitating at the sticking point
Most closes fail in the last 5mm. If you pause to "see if you can," you've lost. Accelerate through the close, mentally aim past it.
Death-gripping warm-ups
Mashing the Trainer 50 times burns the CNS before your working gripper. Warmups stay sub-maximal.

The Program

A 12-week wave block built around a target gripper. Pick the gripper one level above your current MMS close — that's your "next."

How to pick your target
Set up the block
▶
  • Current MMS: The heaviest gripper you can MMS-close for at least 1 clean rep.
  • Working gripper: One level below current MMS. Used for higher-rep sets.
  • Target gripper: One level above current MMS. Used for cheat sets, negatives, holds.
  • Volume gripper: Two levels below current MMS. Used for warmups and conditioning sets.
Example: You can MMS a No. 1. Working = 0.5 or No. 1. Target = No. 1.5. Volume = Trainer.

The Supporting Cast

Crush grip alone breaks down. These are the assistance pieces that keep the system balanced and accelerate the gripper PRs.

Pinch Grip
Thumb-driven · plate pinch
▶
Pinch trains the thumb opponent — the muscle that anchors the crush at the end of the close. Weak thumbs = handles never quite touch.
How to do it
Two plates, smooth sides out, pinched between thumb and four fingers. Hold for time. Add weight by stacking thinner plates.
Programming
3–5 × 20–45s per hand, 2× week. Progress by adding 2.5 lb or 5 seconds at a time.
Thick Bar / Fat Gripz
Open hand · forearm hypertrophy
▶
Thick-handled holds, carries, and deadlifts force the hand into an open, less mechanically advantageous position. Builds the forearm — particularly the deep flexors — better than any crush work.
Best lifts
  • Thick bar deadlift hold: 5 × 10–20s at 60–80% deadlift.
  • Fat Gripz pullups: sets of 3–5, painful.
  • Fat-handle DB row: 3 × 8–10. Free forearm work.
Wrist Curl / Extension
Wrist stability
▶
A flexed wrist destroys grip. Strong wrist flexors keep it locked under load; strong extensors prevent elbow issues. Do both — most lifters only train flexion.
Programming
Flexion: 3 × 12–20, palms up
Extension: 3 × 15–20, palms down. Lighter — extensors are weak.
Bonus: wrist roller
A wrist roller (rope + weight on a wooden handle) trains rotation under load — closer to what a heavy gripper close actually demands.
Finger Extensors
Antagonist · injury proofing
▶
Closing grippers trains the hand to flex. Without antagonist work, you develop the grip-athlete claw and eventually elbow pain. Extensors restore balance and are absurdly cheap to train.
How
Rubber bands around fingers (or a dedicated hand expander). Open the hand against resistance.
3 × 20–25 reps, every gripper session.
Skip this and you'll regret it. Lifters with 5+ years of grip work and no extensor balance get medial epicondylitis ("golfer's elbow"). Cheap insurance.
Farmer's Carry & Dead Hangs
Endurance grip
▶
Crush is maximal-effort. Carries and hangs build the tendon resilience and endurance that lets you handle frequent gripper sessions without flare-ups.
Carry
Heaviest DBs you can hold. 3 × 30–60s or 30–50m. 1–2× week. Strap-free.
Hang
Dead hang from pullup bar, full ROM. 3 × 30–60s. Excellent shoulder decompression bonus.

Test Day Protocol

One real attempt at a new gripper, run right. The difference between an honest test and a sloppy one is often the gripper itself.

The night before

Eat. Sleep 8 hours. No new training stimulus in the past 48 hours. No alcohol. Mild caffeine the morning of is fine.

Warm-up, 15 minutes total

Shake the hands out. Forearm massage / band stretch. Volume gripper × 8 easy. Volume gripper × 5 medium. Working gripper × 3. Working gripper × 1 (smooth, fast). Rest 3 min. Then attempt.

The attempt

Set the gripper deliberately. Take your time on the set — the rep is fast. Squeeze through the close like you're trying to crush past it, not just touch. Hold for 2 seconds (more than the 1 required). Eyes on the handles.

Failed attempt? Rules:

Rest at least 4 minutes. Do NOT keep banging at it. You get a maximum of 5 attempts per session. After the 5th failure, you're done — try again in 4–6 weeks after another block, not next week.

Verifying your close
For honest tracking
▶
  • Film it. Phone propped, front-on. You'll catch sneaky off-hand sets and shallow palm placement that you don't feel.
  • No table contact. Hands free in space during the squeeze. Pressing the bottom handle on a table is a deep set.
  • Parallel set is visible. Pause and verify handles are parallel to your forearm BEFORE the squeeze begins.
  • Held for a full second. Touch-and-pop counts as a brush, not a close. Held = closed.
  • Same gripper both attempts. Don't switch between a "soft" and a "hard" production gripper mid-block. Pick one and stay with it.
Want to certify?
IronMind requirements
▶
IronMind (the maker of CoC) offers official certification at No. 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, 3.5, and 4. Requirements change over time, but typically:
  • Witnessed by a credentialed referee, or filmed to spec.
  • Single attempt with each hand (for some levels), MMS only.
  • Gripper provided/calibrated by IronMind for the attempt.
  • Hold the close clearly for the required duration.
Check IronMind's current rules directly before any certification attempt — they evolve.

Plateaus & Fixes

If progress stops, the answer is almost always one of these five problems.

"I can almost close it but stop in the last 5mm"
End-range weakness
▶
The fix
  • Credit-card sets, 4 × 3 with target gripper.
  • Overcrushes: close working gripper, hold 5–10s × 4 sets.
  • Negatives with target gripper: 4 × 1 (8s eccentric).
2–3 weeks of dedicated end-range work usually unlocks the close.
"I can't even start the squeeze"
Set / starting strength
▶
The fix
  • You set too shallow. Replant with the heel of the palm.
  • You skipped a gripper level — back off to one that's actually trainable.
  • Drop to working gripper, train 5 × 5 MMS for 3–4 weeks.
"I'm getting elbow pain"
Medial epicondylitis
▶
The fix
  • Stop crush work for 1–2 weeks. No exceptions.
  • Heavy finger extensor work (3 × 20–25 daily with bands).
  • Reverse wrist curls 3 × 15–20.
  • Reduce gripper frequency to 2× week max for the next block.
  • Check your set — wrist flexion under load is a primary cause.
Persistent pain > 2 weeks of deload → see a sports physio. Tendinopathy compounds.
"My grip is fine but my hand cramps"
Coordination / recruitment
▶
The fix
  • Cramping is usually a sign of overactive thumb / underactive pinky.
  • Drill pinky-low sets on a lighter gripper, 5 × 8 with deliberate placement.
  • Add finger extensor work (antagonist relaxation).
  • Magnesium and hydration. Boring but real.
"I've stalled for months"
Systemic plateau
▶
The fix
  • Deload 10–14 days. No gripper work. Just pinch and extensors.
  • Switch to a different gripper unit (production variance is real).
  • Re-shoot your set on video and audit it ruthlessly.
  • Add one new accessory: wrist roller, or thick-bar holds.
  • Increase calories and sleep. Grip strength is metabolically expensive.

FAQ & Glossary

Loose ends — questions, myths, and the vocabulary of the grip world.

"How often should I train grippers?"
Frequency
▶
Phase 1: 3×/week. Phase 2: 2–3×/week. Phase 3: 2×/week. Peaking: 1–2×/week. Daily gripper work is almost always wrong — even when it feels fine in the moment, the tendons need recovery.
"Should I train both hands?"
Bilateral
▶
Yes. Off-hand will lag by 1–2 levels but train it identically. Asymmetric grip eventually shows up in elbow/shoulder issues. Cert attempts often require both hands.
"Will deadlifts help my grippers?"
Crossover
▶
Modestly. Deadlift grip is a static support grip — very different from the dynamic crush a gripper demands. Strap-free deadlifts build forearms and resilience, but a 600 lb deadlifter often can't close a No. 2. Train the gripper to close grippers.
"Are the grippers really inconsistent?"
Production variance
▶
Yes. ±5–10% is the norm even from IronMind, and across other brands variance is wider. If you fail one gripper consistently, try a different unit of the same rating — sometimes you've just got an unusually "hard" specimen. Calibrated grippers exist for certification.
"Do I need chalk?"
Friction
▶
For test attempts and heavy work: yes. Sweaty hands slip the handle deeper into the palm at the wrong angle. A small amount on the heel of the palm is enough. Train sometimes without chalk to keep skin tough.
"Grip work or full-body work first?"
Sequencing
▶
Grippers first if you care about the close. Heavy pulling fatigues the forearms and you'll under-perform on grippers right after. If you train grippers on a separate day from your main pulls, even better.
Glossary
Speak the language
▶
MMS — Mean Metallic Set
Handles set parallel to the forearm with no assist. The certifiable close.
CCS — Credit Card Set
Credit card placed between handles before squeezing. Training tool, not a real close.
TNS — The No Set
Pick up the gripper and close it from however it lands. Strictest possible standard.
Negative
Closing with assistance, then slowly resisting the open. Heavy eccentric.
Overcrush
Holding a closed gripper shut under tension for 3–10 seconds. End-range training.
Cert / Certification
Official IronMind-recognized close of a No. 1.5 or higher gripper.
Crush grip
The closing-the-hand-against-resistance pattern. What CoC grippers train.
Pinch grip
Thumb-opposed grip across a flat object (plates). Separate skill from crush.
Support grip
Static hold like a deadlift or farmer's carry. Endurance-biased.
Open hand grip
Thick-bar or wide-object grip with fingers not fully closed. Thick-handled holds train this.
Connecting Garmin later
Recovery-gated training
▶
When Garmin is connected, the following data points become useful for tuning this program:
  • HRV / Body Battery low → substitute a heavy day for a volume day or skip.
  • Training load spiking → cut grip frequency to 2× that week.
  • Poor sleep score → don't attempt a new MMS PR. Move test day.
  • Resting HR elevated → CNS is taxed; prioritize technique drills over heavy negatives.
Crush grip is highly neural. Recovery data predicts max-effort performance better than soreness does.
