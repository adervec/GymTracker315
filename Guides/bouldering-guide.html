Bouldering rewards patience over power, footwork over forearms, and the climber who learns to think before they pull. What follows is the long answer to a short question — how do I get better?

§ 01 / FOUR

Movement, first.

01.

Use your feet first.

Most beginners over-rely on arm strength. Place your feet precisely and trust them — your legs are far stronger than your arms. Look at a foothold, place your toe (not the middle of your foot) on it deliberately, and don't shift your eyes away until it's set.

Footwork V0+
02.

Hang from your skeleton.

Bent arms burn out fast. Straighten your arms between moves and let your bones bear the load instead of your biceps. Conserve energy for the moments when you actually need to pull.

Endurance V1+
03.

Hips to the wall.

Get your hips close to the wall to keep weight over your feet. Turning a hip into the wall — a drop knee, or simply twisting your pelvis — extends your reach and reduces strain on your fingers and arms.

Body Position V2+
04.

Move quietly.

Loud, scrabbling feet signal poor precision. Aim for silent, intentional foot placements. If you can hear yourself climb, you can climb better.

Precision V3+
§ 02 / FOUR

Train like a problem.

REST

Climb often, but rest.

Two to four sessions per week with full rest days between is plenty for most people. Tendons and skin recover slower than muscles — respect that gap.

MIX

Project and flash.

Some sessions, flash easier problems for volume and technique. Other sessions, work harder problems at your limit for strength and problem-solving.

LOOK

Climb with better climbers.

Watch their beta. Ask questions. Try to mimic their movement before you try to match their grade. Most of bouldering is invisible until you see it done well.

WARM

Warm up properly.

Start with easy traverses, then climb progressively harder problems before attempting anything near your max. Cold fingers injure first and heal slowest.

§ 03 / FOUR

A vocabulary of moves.

Skill Why it matters
Reading routes Plan sequences from the ground. You save energy and avoid the panic of mid-route hesitation.
Body tension Holds you on steep and overhung walls. Without it, your feet cut every time you reach.
Heel & toe hooks Use feet as a third hand. Pull with your legs, not just push.
Flagging Counterbalance with a free leg to stay stable instead of barn-dooring off the wall.
Dynamic moves Reach holds that static movement can't. Commit, then catch — hesitation is the failure mode.
§ 04 / FOUR

The thing that ends seasons.

Caution / Fingers, Elbows, Skin

Injury is the only thing that actually sets you back.

Finger injuries are the most common setback in bouldering. Don't crimp aggressively early on — use open-hand grips when possible, and stop immediately if you feel sharp pain in a finger or elbow. Pain you can climb through is rarely the kind you should.

Build finger strength gradually, over months and years, not weeks. Hangboarding is generally not recommended until you've climbed consistently for at least a year. Your tendons are on a different timeline than your motivation.

CODA

On plateaus.

Progress is not linear. It arrives in bursts, then disappears for weeks. Climb anyway.

Plateaus are normal. Progress comes in bursts followed by flat stretches that feel longer than they are. Film yourself climbing to spot inefficiencies you can't feel in the moment — you'll be surprised how different your climbing looks from the outside.

Focus on movement quality over grade-chasing. The climber obsessed with the next number tends to reinforce bad habits and collect injuries. The climber obsessed with moving well tends to find the numbers anyway.

Send slow.
